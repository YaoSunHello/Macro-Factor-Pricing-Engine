## ERA 1: 1900–1913 — PRE-WWI BOOM, PANIC OF 1907

### Macro Context
- US GDP growing ~4% real annually; industrialization accelerating
- Gold standard anchors currencies; UK pound dominant reserve currency
- No central bank in US (Federal Reserve created 1913)
- Railroad, steel, and trust expansion; J.P. Morgan era of private credit intermediation

### Asset Environment
| Asset Class | Regime |
|---|---|
| Equities | Bull market; DJIA rose ~125% 1900–1906 |
| Bonds | Low yields; 10Y UST ~3.5–4.0% |
| Commodities | Commodity boom (copper, coal, railroad inputs) |
| USD | Stable under gold standard |
| Real Estate | Urban expansion, rising land values |
| Equity-Bond Correlation | Positive (both rising in boom) |

### EVENT: PANIC OF 1907

**WHAT Happened**
- DJIA fell ~48% peak-to-trough (Jan 1906–Nov 1907); ~15-month drawdown
- Bank runs across New York trust companies (October 1907)
- Knickerbocker Trust collapse triggered contagion (Oct 22, 1907)
- Credit markets seized; call money rates spiked to 100%+ overnight
- Stock market threatened with complete closure; NYSE nearly suspended trading
- Recovery: DJIA fully recovered by ~1909 (~2 years)

**WHY It Happened**
- Over-leveraged speculative positions in copper (Heinze/Morse copper corner attempt failed)
- Trust companies operating outside National Banking Act — no reserve requirements, no lender of last resort
- San Francisco earthquake (April 1906) drained gold reserves, tightened credit nationwide
- British banks recalled gold loans, amplifying US liquidity squeeze
- No central bank to inject reserves; private banking system entirely self-reliant

**LEADING INDICATORS (6–18 months prior)**
- Copper price collapse (-50% from peak) in early 1907 — commodity bust leading equities
- Call money rates rising sharply through 1907
- Trust company leverage ratios at historic highs
- British current account surplus falling; gold outflows from UK to US reversing
- Equity market peaked Jan 1906 — 21 months before bank panic

**CONCURRENT/LAGGING INDICATORS**
- Bank failures cascading (3 major trusts in 2 weeks)
- Call money to 100%+ signals complete liquidity seizure
- Gold premium emerging (currency credibility questioned)

**CROSS-ASSET IMPACT**
- Equities: -48% DJIA
- Credit: Complete seizure; corporate bond spreads exploded
- Commodities: Copper -50%, broader industrial commodities fell 20–30%
- FX: Dollar remained gold-pegged but gold hoarding emerged
- Real Estate: Urban land values fell 10–20% in affected cities

**WHAT ENDED IT**
- J.P. Morgan organized private banking consortium; injected $25M+ into clearing houses
- US Treasury deposited $25M in New York banks
- Morgan forced banks to cooperate; issued clearing house certificates as quasi-money
- Crisis lasted ~3 weeks at peak intensity; equity recovery over 2 years

**PATTERN SIGNATURE**
- Unregulated leverage + commodity speculation bust + no lender of last resort
- Trust/shadow banking system at epicenter (not regulated banks)
- Recovery fast once private backstop established — precedent for central bank function

## ERA 2: 1914–1918 — WWI MARKET DISRUPTIONS

**WHAT Happened**
- NYSE closed July 31, 1914 – December 12, 1914 (~4.5 months) — longest closure in history
- Closure prevented foreign capital flight and gold drain
- When reopened, DJIA initially fell ~30% then recovered sharply by 1916
- DJIA rose ~80% 1914–1919 as US became global arms/food supplier
- US shifted from debtor to creditor nation; gold flowed in massively

### Asset Environment
| Asset Class | Behavior |
|---|---|
| Equities | Initial crash → strong bull market as war profiteering boomed |
| Bonds | Liberty Bonds issued; yields rose as war financing needs grew |
| Commodities | Massive boom: wheat, steel, coal, copper all surged 50–200% |
| USD | Strengthened dramatically as gold inflows accumulated |
| Inflation | CPI rose ~100% cumulative 1914–1920 (war + commodity boom) |
| Equity-Bond Correlation | Negative during peak war years (bonds fell as equities rose) |

**KEY MECHANISMS**
- US neutrality until 1917 → pure beneficiary of war demand
- Liberty Bond campaigns ($21.5B raised) created mass retail bond investing
- Federal Reserve Act (1913) gave new central bank tools; used discount window heavily
- Gold standard suspended by European belligerents → USD became de facto reserve
- Post-war: Commodity bust 1920–21 as war demand collapsed; stocks fell 45%

**LEADING INDICATORS FOR POST-WAR BUST (1920–21)**
- Commodity prices peaked summer 1920 and collapsed
- Fed raised discount rate to 7% (June 1920) — sharpest tightening since founding
- Inventory accumulation at record levels as war demand ended
- Recession lasted 18 months but V-shaped; no banking crisis

## ERA 4: 1929–1933 — GREAT DEPRESSION

### WHAT Happened
**Equity destruction:**
- DJIA: 381.2 (Sep 3, 1929) → 41.22 (Jul 8, 1932) = **-89.2% in 34 months**
- Not fully recovered until November 1954 — **25 years**

**Timeline of crashes:**
- Black Thursday: Oct 24, 1929 (-11% intraday)
- Black Monday: Oct 28, 1929 (-13.5%)
- Black Tuesday: Oct 29, 1929 (-11.7%)
- Intermediate rally: Nov 1929 – Apr 1930 (+50%)
- Second leg down: Apr 1930 – Jul 1932 (-84% from peak)
- Third crash: 1937 recession (-49% from Sep 1937 – Mar 1938)

**Economic destruction:**
- US real GDP fell 29% (1929–1933)
- Unemployment: 3.2% (1929) → 24.9% (1933)
- ~9,000 banks failed
- Money supply (M2) contracted 33%
- Deflation: CPI fell ~10% cumulative
- Global trade collapsed 66% (Smoot-Hawley tariff + retaliation)

### WHY It Happened (Multi-causal)
1. **Fed policy failure**: Fed raised rates 1928–29 to stop speculation; then failed to act as lender of last resort during bank runs (Friedman/Schwartz thesis)
2. **Margin call cascade**: 10:1 margin ratios meant 10% market drop wiped out equity; forced selling amplified decline
3. **Bank fragility**: Unit banking system (no branching); banks failed in waves
4. **Money supply contraction**: Each bank failure destroyed deposits; money multiplier collapsed
5. **Smoot-Hawley Tariff** (June 1930): Raised tariffs on 20,000 goods; triggered retaliatory tariffs; global trade -66%
6. **Gold standard straitjacket**: Countries couldn't reflate; deflationary spiral self-reinforcing
7. **Debt-deflation**: Rising real debt burdens as prices fell → bankruptcies → more deflation (Irving Fisher, 1933)
8. **Hoover's balanced-budget obsession**: Fiscal tightening in recession amplified contraction

### LEADING INDICATORS (available in real time)
| Signal | Status Pre-Crash |
|---|---|
| Yield curve | Flat to inverted (Fed tightening) |
| Margin debt/GDP | Record high |
| P/E ratios | 30–33x (2x historical average) |
| Agricultural commodity prices | Multi-year downtrend |
| Florida real estate | Already collapsed (1926) |
| Steel/auto production | Turning down mid-1929 |
| A/D line breadth | Diverging negatively |
| Rural bank failures | Accelerating 1927–29 |

### CROSS-ASSET IMPACT
| Asset | Move | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Equities (DJIA) | -89.2% | 34 months down; 25 years to recover |
| Corporate bonds | High-grade: -20%; Junk: -80%+ | Multi-year |
| Treasuries | RALLY (flight to safety) 10Y fell from 3.8% to 3.0% | Throughout depression |
| Commodities | CRB-equivalent: -60%+ | Deflationary collapse |
| Real estate | -25–30% nationally; some urban markets -50%+ | 5–10 years |
| USD | Strong initially (gold inflows); devalued 40% in 1933 | Structural shift |
| Gold | Revalued upward: $20.67 → $35/oz (April 1933, +69%) | Permanent until 1971 |

**EQUITY-BOND CORRELATION: NEGATIVE** — Classic flight to safety; bonds were the only safe haven

### WHAT ENDED IT
1. Roosevelt elected (Nov 1932) — confidence shift
2. Bank Holiday (March 1933) — stopped bank run cascade
3. Glass-Steagall (June 1933) — FDIC insurance, separating commercial/investment banking
4. Gold standard abandoned (April 1933) — FDR Executive Order 6102; devalued dollar
5. New Deal fiscal spending (1933–1937): CCC, WPA, TVA
6. 1937 premature tightening caused second recession; ultimately WWII spending ended depression

### PATTERN SIGNATURE (Depression Template)
- Asset price bubble + excessive margin leverage → forced selling cascade
- Banking crisis amplifying through money supply contraction
- Deflationary spiral: price declines → real debt burden rises → more defaults
- Policy mistake (tight money + fiscal austerity) extending the crisis
- **Recovery requires**: Lender of last resort + deposit insurance + currency devaluation + fiscal stimulus

- Post-war deferred consumption + pent-up savings = boom conditions

## ERA 7: 1966–1971 — LATE 60s INFLATION, NIXON SHOCK

### WHAT Happened
- DJIA sideways 1966–1982 in NOMINAL terms (~750–1050 range); -70% in REAL terms
- 1966 mini-crash: -25% (Feb–Oct 1966)
- 1968–1969 tightening: -36% (Dec 1968 – May 1970)

**WHY:**
- Vietnam War spending + Great Society social programs → "guns and butter" fiscal excess
- Johnson refused to raise taxes → deficit spending → inflation
- Fed accommodated (Arthur Burns era) → monetary credibility undermined
- Inflation: 1.7% (1965) → 6.2% (1969)

### Nixon Shock (August 15, 1971)
- Suspended dollar-gold convertibility ($35/oz fixed rate)
- Imposed 10% import surcharge + 90-day wage and price controls
- **Ended Bretton Woods system**

**CROSS-ASSET IMPACT of Nixon Shock:**
| Asset | Immediate Response | 1-Year Response |
|---|---|---|
| Gold | +8% immediately; ultimately $35 → $850 (1980) = +2,329% | Multi-year surge |
| USD | -10% trade-weighted in 6 months | Structural bear begins |
| Equities | Initially rallied +4% (relief) | Gave back in stagflation |
| Commodities | Surge began; CRB Index eventually +300% by 1980 | Commodity supercycle start |
| Bonds | Yields rose as inflation expectations untethered | Multi-year bear |

**LEADING INDICATORS of Bretton Woods Collapse:**
- US gold reserves falling for 20 years as dollar claims accumulated
- "Dollar overhang" — foreign dollar holdings >> US gold reserves
- Balance of payments deficits widening
- Triffin Dilemma: reserve currency must run deficits to supply liquidity → inevitable credibility crisis

## ERA 8: 1971–1982 — STAGFLATION, OIL SHOCKS, VOLCKER SHOCK

### Macro Environment
- Inflation: 4% (1971) → 14.8% (March 1980)
- Fed Funds Rate: 3.5% (1971) → 20.06% (June 1981)
- Two recessions: 1973–75 and 1980–82
- DJIA (nominal): ~850 in 1966 → ~875 in 1982 (flat for 16 years, -70% real)

### EVENT: OIL SHOCK I (1973)

**WHAT Happened:**
- Arab OPEC oil embargo (Oct 1973 – Mar 1974) following Yom Kippur War
- Oil price: $3/barrel → $12/barrel = **+300%** in 6 months
- S&P 500 fell **-48.2%** (Jan 1973 – Oct 1974) — 21 months
- GDP fell 3.2% real; unemployment rose to 9%
- "Stagflation" coined: simultaneous high inflation + high unemployment + slow growth

**LEADING INDICATORS:**
- Geopolitical tensions in Middle East rising
- US domestic oil production peaked (1970) — Hubbert's Peak realized
- Nixon wage/price controls distorting price signals
- Dollar devaluation (1971) raising commodity prices globally
- M2 money supply growing 10–12% annually

**CROSS-ASSET IMPACT:**
| Asset | Move |
|---|---|
| S&P 500 | -48.2% |
| Treasuries | Yields rose (5% → 8%); prices fell |
| Oil | +300% |
| Gold | $35 → $185 (+429%) by end 1974 |
| USD | -15% trade-weighted |
| Real estate | Held up initially (inflation hedge) |
| Credit | Spreads widened 200–300bps |

**EQUITY-BOND CORRELATION: POSITIVE (both falling)** — classic stagflationary regime

### EVENT: OIL SHOCK II (1979)

**WHAT Happened:**
- Iranian Revolution (1979) removed Iran from oil markets
- Oil: $13/barrel → $34/barrel = **+162%** (1978–1980)
- Inflation peaked at **14.8%** (March 1980)

### VOLCKER SHOCK (October 1979–1982)
- Fed Funds: 10% → **20.06%** (June 1981)
- Two recessions: Jan–Jul 1980 (-2.2% GDP); Jul 1981–Nov 1982 (-2.6% GDP)
- Unemployment peaked at **10.8%** (December 1982)
- Inflation: 14.8% → 3% (1983) — credible disinflation achieved

**WHAT ENDED STAGFLATION:**
- Two deep recessions broke wage-price spiral
- Oil prices collapsed (1985–86): Saudi Arabia abandoned OPEC quota; oil $30 → $10
- Dollar soared 50% (1980–1985) → disinflation via import prices
- Supply-side: Reagan deregulation + tax cuts → productivity improvement

**PATTERN SIGNATURE (Stagflation Template):**
- Energy supply shock + loose monetary policy + fiscal excess = stagflation
- Cannot be fixed by demand stimulus alone
- Requires credible monetary tightening (accepting recession/unemployment)
- Duration: ~8–10 years from onset to resolution
- Equity-bond correlation **POSITIVE** (both fall) — most dangerous portfolio regime

### EVENT: BLACK MONDAY — OCTOBER 19, 1987

**WHAT Happened:**
- DJIA fell **-22.6% in a single day** — largest single-day % decline ever
- S&P 500: -33% peak-to-trough (Aug–Dec 1987)
- Full recovery: January 1989 (~15 months)
- No recession followed

**WHY It Happened:**
1. **Portfolio insurance**: Options-based hedging → as market fell, programs sold futures → feedback loop
2. **Interest rate spike**: 10Y Treasury yield rose from 7.5% to 10% in Aug–Oct 1987
3. **Dollar weakness**: Louvre Accord broke down; inflation fears
4. **Valuation**: P/E ratio at ~20x+ at peak
5. **Program trading**: Emerging computerized trading amplified moves

**LEADING INDICATORS (2–6 months prior):**
- Bond yields rising sharply through Q3 1987 (+250bps in 3 months)
- Dollar weakness persisting despite G7 coordination
- P/E ratio at cycle highs
- Advance-decline diverging negatively
- Rising VXO in weeks before crash

**CROSS-ASSET IMPACT:**
| Asset | Move |
|---|---|
| S&P 500 | -33.5% peak to trough |
| Treasuries | RALLIED (flight to safety) — 10Y yield fell 150bps |
| Gold | Rose +4% (modest safe haven) |

**WHAT ENDED IT:**
- Alan Greenspan issued statement: Fed would provide liquidity
- Fed cut rates and injected reserves; "Greenspan Put" born
- Economy fundamentally healthy → full S&P recovery in ~15 months

**PATTERN SIGNATURE:**
- Market structure/mechanical failure amplifying fundamental stress
- Rising rates + elevated valuations = crash risk
- Fed backstop = faster recovery; no recession = V-shaped recovery

## ERA 10: 1987–1994 — S&L CRISIS, GULF WAR, 1994 BOND MASSACRE

### Savings & Loan Crisis
- 1,043 of ~3,234 S&L institutions failed (1986–1995)
- Total cost: ~$160B ($87B to taxpayers)
- RTC created 1989; real estate in affected markets fell 20–30%
- 1990 recession (-1.4% GDP) partly driven by S&L credit crunch

### Gulf War I (1990–1991)
- Iraq invaded Kuwait (August 2, 1990)
- S&P 500 fell -19.9% (Jul–Oct 1990)
- Oil: $15 → $40/barrel = +167% (Aug–Oct 1990)
- Operation Desert Storm (Jan 17, 1991): War started → markets immediately rallied
- S&P full recovery by March 1991

**PATTERN: War/Geopolitical Shock**
- Market bottomed BEFORE war started (buying certainty vs. uncertainty of threat)
- "Sell the rumor, buy the fact"

### 1994 BOND MASSACRE

**WHAT Happened:**
- 30Y Treasury yield: 5.8% (Jan 1994) → 8.05% (Dec 1994) = **+225bps in 12 months**
- US bond market lost ~$1 trillion in value
- S&P 500: -8.9% (Jan–Apr 1994) — limited equity damage
- Orange County, CA bankruptcy (December 1994): $1.7B loss on leveraged rate bets
- Mexican Peso crisis (December 1994): peso devalued 50%, EM contagion

**WHY:**
- Fed held rates at 3% since 1992; February 4, 1994: first hike in 5 years (25bps shock)
- 7 more hikes in 1994: Funds rate 3% → 5.5%
- Duration extension by bond managers made portfolios extremely rate-sensitive
- Derivative leverage (CMOs, inverse floaters) amplified losses

**PATTERN SIGNATURE (Rate Surprise Template):**
- Extended low rates → leverage/duration extension → rate surprise → violent repricing
- Bond crash does not necessarily cause equity crash if growth is good
- EM contagion: capital flows reverse when US rates rise sharply

## ERA 11: 1994–2000 — DOTCOM BUBBLE, LTCM, ASIAN CRISIS, RUSSIAN DEFAULT

### Asian Financial Crisis (1997)

**WHAT Happened:**
- Thai baht devaluation (July 2, 1997) — speculative attacks broke the peg
- Contagion: Indonesia, South Korea, Malaysia, Philippines currencies crashed 30–80%
- South Korean won: -54%; Indonesian rupiah: -83%
- S&P 500: Dipped -10.8% (Aug–Oct 1997) then recovered

**WHY:**
- Current account deficits funded by short-term dollar debt (currency mismatch)
- Fixed exchange rate regimes overvalued; bank systems had hidden NPLs
- Hot money inflows reversed as US rates rose

**LEADING INDICATORS:**
- Current account deficits widening in Thailand, Indonesia, Korea
- Short-term dollar debt >> foreign reserves
- Real effective exchange rates overvalued by 20–40%
- Property bubbles in Bangkok, Jakarta

### LTCM (Long-Term Capital Management) Crisis — 1998

**WHAT Happened:**
- Russian default (August 17, 1998): Government defaulted on ruble debt
- LTCM lost 44% in August alone; down 52% for 1998
- Fed-coordinated bailout: 14 banks contributed $3.65B rescue
- LTCM had $125B in assets, $1.25T in notional derivatives; leverage ~25:1
- S&P 500: -19.8% (Jul–Oct 1998)
- 10Y Treasury yield fell from 5.5% to 4.5% (flight to safety) in 6 weeks

**WHY LTCM Failed:**
1. Model risk: Models assumed normal distributions; actually fat tails in crisis
2. Correlation assumption: Assumed uncorrelated positions; crisis caused all to correlate
3. Liquidity assumption: Assumed could exit positions; in crisis, no liquidity
4. Convergence trades: Russia default caused divergence, not convergence
5. Leverage: 25:1+ leverage meant 4% adverse move = 100% equity loss

**PATTERN SIGNATURE (Leverage Unwind):**
- All correlations go to 1 in a crisis
- On-the-run vs. off-the-run Treasury spread (normally ~5bps) went to 30bps
- Fed cut 75bps total (Sept–Nov 1998); recovery fast — no real economy damage

**CROSS-ASSET IMPACT (LTCM/Russia 1998):**
| Asset | Move |
|---|---|
| US Equities | -19.8% |
| Treasuries | 10Y yield -100bps (massive rally) |
| EM bonds | +600–1000bps spread |
| High yield credit | +500bps |
| Gold | +5% |

## ERA 13: 2003–2007 — HOUSING BUBBLE, CREDIT EXPANSION, CARRY TRADES

### Macro Environment
- S&P 500: 800 (Oct 2002) → 1,576 (Oct 2007) = **+97% in 5 years**
- US housing prices (Case-Shiller National): +89% cumulative (1997–2006)
- CDOs, CLOs, MBS, ABS-CDO ("CDO-squared") explosion
- Global carry trade: Borrow in JPY at 0%, invest in EM/US high yield at 5–8%
- China GDP growing 11–13%/year

### BUBBLE ANATOMY — LEADING INDICATORS (visible in 2005–2006)

**Lending standards deterioration:**
- NINJA loans (No Income, No Job, No Assets)
- LTV ratios rising to 100%+
- Subprime originations: $190B (2001) → $600B (2006) = +216%
- Adjustable rate mortgages: 35%+ of new originations by 2006

**Delinquency leading indicators:**
- 2006 vintage subprime mortgages showing elevated early-payment defaults
- ABX index (credit default swaps on subprime MBS) declining H2 2006
- Home price appreciation decelerating/reversing in Phoenix, Las Vegas, Miami (2006)
- Housing starts peaked February 2006

**Credit structure warning signs:**
- AAA-rated CDO tranches: models assumed uncorrelated subprime defaults (wrong)
- Triple-A ratings on instruments backed by single-B loans (ratings agency failure)
- OTC derivatives: no central clearing, no price transparency

### Equity-Bond Correlation Regimes

| Period | Regime | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| 1900–1913 | Positive | Gold standard stability; both in growth regime |
